Task 2

For the following undirected graph:

1: [3],
2: [1, 4],
3: [1, 4, 5],
4: [6, 7],
5: [6, 2],
6: [4, 5],
7: [8],
8: [7]

Note: There's an inconsistency in the given adjacency list. Correcting it to be symmetric for undirected graph:

1: [3, 2],
2: [1, 4, 5],
3: [1, 4, 5],
4: [2, 3, 6, 7],
5: [3, 2, 6],
6: [4, 5],
7: [4, 8],
8: [7]

Answers:

  1. How many vertices and edges are there in the graph?
  2. Vertices: 8
  3. Edges: 9 (counting each edge once: (1,2), (1,3), (2,4), (2,5), (3,4), (3,5), (4,6), (4,7), (5,6), (6,5) = duplicate, (7,8))

  4. Corrected count: 8 edges

  5. Is the graph connected? Justify your answer.

  6. Yes, the graph is connected. All vertices can be reached from any other vertex through a path.

  7. What is the diameter of the graph?

  8. The diameter is 4 (longest shortest path, e.g., from vertex 8 to vertex 1: 8→7→4→2→1)

  9. What is the radius of the graph?

  10. The radius is 2 (minimum eccentricity among all vertices)

  11. Which vertex (or vertices) is in the center of the graph?

  12. Vertices 2, 3, 4 are in the center (vertices with eccentricity equal to radius)

  13. Is the graph Eulerian? Why or why not?

  14. No, the graph is not Eulerian because vertices 1 and 8 have odd degree (degree 1), while Eulerian graphs require all vertices to have even degree.

  15. Find the degree of vertex 4.

  16. Degree of vertex 4: 4 (connected to vertices 2, 3, 6, 7)

  17. Find the shortest path from vertex 5 to vertex 8 using Dijkstra's algorithm.

  18. Shortest path: 5 → 2 → 4 → 7 → 8

  19. What is the length of the shortest path from vertex 5 to vertex 8?

  20. Length: 4

  21. Does the graph contain a cycle? If yes, what is the length of one cycle?

    • Yes, the graph contains cycles. One example: 2 → 4 → 3 → 5 → 2, which has length 4.

Task 3

Consider the above graph as a directed graph. In this case, the adjacency list represents directed edges:

1: [3],
2: [1, 4],
3: [1, 4, 5],
4: [6, 7],
5: [6, 2],
6: [4, 5],
7: [8],
8: [7]

For a directed graph interpretation: - The graph may not be strongly connected - Some vertices may not be reachable from others - The analysis would need to consider directed paths only - Eulerian properties would require all vertices to have equal in-degree and out-degree
